Turning Pages: Embracing Change as the Next Chapter of Your Journey

TTwo weeks ago I went up to my apartment’s rooftop to get a whiff of fresh air, ‘work-out’ a bit and get some peace. Everything was in its place as usual; the six rarely-used and abandoned dumbbells were in the far left corner, the lone DSTV dish and the antennae for the problematic building Wi-Fi were all in place, except the whole place felt different that day. Maybe it was the foul smell from the protruding toilet vents or the fact that all the hanging lines were empty on a Saturday, or the fact that I did not want to climb to the rooftop in the first place but still did. Before I could craft up more theories for the general aura, I saw my first clue; from a lens view, the birds that fly above in circles whenever I went there were hurriedly flapping south, on top of that, there was a huge nimbus above but instead of raining it just was accompanied by ‘ruin some good makeup’ kind of wind, and the environment was a little bit darker for 5 PM. My general conclusion was that climate seasons were changing and this was not good for me.

I did take Geography in High School and if my memory serves me right then we are sort of experiencing longer days and shorter nights as the sun is slightly overhead the tropic of Cancer or something like that, I forgot the nitty-gritty. The point is, the season was changing. I had gotten used to seeing the sunset drop behind the glorious Ngong hills as I cracked my shoulders trying to lift the 30 kg weights. In some way, the gold-coloured sunsets were a consolation for the excruciating pain of trying to get into shape. My main concern was what was going to happen! What did this mean for me and my ‘rooftop time’ habit? Am I going to do away with the habit I had worked so hard to craft and standardise because evenings are now colder and windier? And if I do, who knows how much willpower, physical strength and determination it would take to reinstate my sunset routine? Yes, I have always been against change for most of my life.

Sometimes I want to argue that If it ain’t broken then don’t fix it, or change it for that matter. This is because growing accustomed to one system of life or a way of things gives me a certain feeling of control over things, and predictability, a certain know-how of how to navigate around situations and this, in turn, brings me peace. Human beings like to feel in control and when things start changing unpredictably for that matter, it raises our anxiety and in my case, messed up my daily routine. Don't get me wrong, change is subjective as we tend to adapt quickly to positive change as compared to negative change. Needless to say, we have different rates of adaptation; some find a thrill in the novelty of things and change wouldn’t be that much of an obstacle. But let me also shine a light on the punch that knocks you out, the one you don't see coming - Willie Pastrano. I am talking about the ‘hard to walk from or navigate around’ kind of change, for example, the idea of losing someone and having to adapt to life without them, the idea of losing a job and having to start over, and quitting something because it was not working out, you are never prepared for these kinds of things. But here is the reassuring part.

Change was not invented, it is existential. Let’s temporarily reverse time to events like the Spanish Flu (1918), the beginning of the HIV/AIDS epidemic, and more recently, the COVID-19 pandemic. All of these incidents have reshaped human interaction, healthcare systems, economies, and societal behaviour; They were unprecedented, and the effects were devastating but the research and input in controlling and managing these crises have evolved the medical sector and the general public hygiene measures. If I was to dive into the complexities and the different types of change I will end up writing a whole e-book so let me stick to my ambidextrous approach to convince you to learn to embrace this ice-cold phenomenon.

I am going to put a focus on one of my areas of struggle in regards to change and that is resisting it, especially when it is unprecedented. So first, why do we resist change? Well, psychologists say that our bodies and brains are wired to resist change. It takes more energy to adapt to change as it develops new neural pathways and connections. Adapting demands more focus and mental and sometimes physical strength. We stop ourselves from changing because sticking to what we already know is easier; we like to stick to an implanted idea rather than adapt to a changing environment. Medically speaking, when your brain easily adapts to change we say you are neuroplastic; Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to change and adapt due to experience; the brain's neurons have to rewire and reorganise and adapt to a new system.

So what does happen when we don't embrace change and instead resist it? Well for starters: We will end up playing catch game. I mean think about it, in the corporate world, the obvious and ever-present technological advancements, I mean you blink and there is already a new AI that can do your job. Eventually, you adapt to it despite efforts to remain in your comfort zone. Change is the bitter pill we just have to swallow and hope that it gets habitual with time. Ancient Eastern philosopher Lao Tzu, the author of Taoism wrote ‘As a rule, whatever is fluid, soft, and yielding will overcome whatever is rigid and hard.’ which is paradoxical as what is soft is strong; being fluid and flexible to change gives you an edge over life’s unplanned circumstances. Lao Tzu’s teachings were all based on the concept of ‘Wu Wei’ which means ‘Effortless Action’. It is an encouragement to live with the natural flow of life instead of resisting it.

The ability to adapt to not only positive change but negative change shows that you have evolved and that you have the right mental attitude to face life. We embrace change because we have no option but to. If it's on the list of things you cannot control, you navigate around it. And yes, I found a way to navigate around my rooftop routine after a long hiatus. At one point you stopped being a child and started taking responsibility, not because you opted to but because you had no other option but to (not speaking for the general masses). Hey, I found a way to workout without sunsets, adapted after being rejected to multiple jobs, I will probably adapt to the imminent high taxation or even harsh weather conditions. I usually have naive-optimism and trust in my ability to handle life's abruot changes and Its all just a mental shift away from your grasp. So don’t stick to one page, instead keep writing. A plot twist always makes the story interesting.

You have an Awesome Week

Peace
