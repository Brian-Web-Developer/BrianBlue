How Our Habits Make Us: Atomic Habits by James Clear

I recently finished reading Atomic Habits by James Clear after months of procrastination, hiatuses, stalemates and sudden breaks. I want to share my most valued lessons from the book and that is ‘how our habits make up who we are’ but before I list down my takeaways from the book, I should probably tell you the decision that led me to pick the book. I am a ratings-based person, I only watch movies or series if they are rated 7/10 and above on IMDB and books if they have good reviews. So it was only logical that when I went all out on reading a self-help book, I would go for the one with the most ratings, or the most recommended one, and this decision did not disappoint.

This book was a good match for the point I am in life, I started highlighting and screenshotting paragraphs from the first page. I also set up the best approach to learn and implement most of the ideas from the book. First, I am more of a visual and auditory learner so for this to be helpful, I had to take screenshots and then later re-write the content into a Google Sheets and have a summary in my notebook. This enhanced my readability and my ability to grasp some of the terms like ‘Implementation Intentions’.

I am a practical reader, that is when I find a book interesting I will try to actualise some of the ideas. With that said, I habituated most of my valued attributes such as not working from my bed, playing only instrumentals when I want to keep focus, the art of opening my project folder even when I don't feel like working, having morning and evening rituals, the art of awareness (which helped in cultivating mindfulness) and generally applying everything I learn to everything around me.

So to round off my experience and knowledge from this book, these are my top picks for the greatest lessons this book has to offer

  • Your Habits will become Boring. The greatest threat to success is not failure but boredom! Habits become more routine and boring as we master them and the ability to stick with them when boredom hits differentiates great achievers from failures. This is the biggest, most controversial and yet the most valuable lesson in this book for me. James outlines how it takes more than just passion, drive and hard work to keep up with a good habit. He explains that the most successful people in habit formation are not the ones with the most passion or drive but those who can handle the boredom that comes with doing the same thing every day. To put this into context, if you are an athlete, a bodybuilder, or a data analyst etc, there are days when you will not feel like showing up, doing a jump, or opening your laptop and that is normal, every human experiences a dip in motivation and it is your ability to pick yourself up from that boredom and still show up that differentiates you from the general public. ‘It doesn’t matter what happens at the workout, it's about you being the person who does not miss a workout’.
  • Your habits make up your identity. This helped shift my mentality and perception around habits. The identity-based approach is the best way to make good habits stick and get rid of bad ones. Here is one of the examples the book gives to differentiate identity-based habits, for instance, two smokers who are trying to quit. One of the smokers when handed a cigarette says ‘I am trying to quit’ and another one says ‘I am not a smoker’. The second statement has more gravitas and it is one of the best ways to develop new habits, to develop identity-based habits. Instead of saying ‘I work out every day’ you could say ‘I am a body builder’, instead of ‘I cook a lot’ you could say ‘ I am a chef’ etc.
  • You can’t grow positive habits in a negative environment. Your general environment, your friends, your mentors and your peers all determine your level of performance in a task, your mental health and your overall growth in general. You cannot expect to quit smoking if all your friends are smokers, you cannot expect to practice mindfulness in an ever-noisy neighbourhood. At some point, you have to put yourself in an environment that brings out the best in you. Studies show that placing a less-performing employee next to a high performer increases the performance of the less-performing one naturally. So to form the best habits and be better at them, put yourself in a ‘tribe’ of the kind of habit you are trying to normalize.
  • The cost of your good habits is immediate, the cost of your bad habits is in the future. Think about it this way, if smoking cigarettes gave you cancer in a week then possibly no one would smoke them, right? It is the immediate gratification and the delayed cost that makes people smoke. So when forming habits, the more immediate the pleasure is from the habit, the more you should question that habit. ‘What is immediately rewarded is repeated, what is immediately punished is avoided’. This is a good mental shift around habits in that the best habits are often those whose gratifications are delayed.
  • Your growth, habits and routines are an endless cycle. Old habits become easier the second time but overall, it does not get easier. Finishing a level is simply unlocking a new harder one (It’s like a game) and therefore the best way to think about habits is to see them as a lifelong process and not a gateway to achieving something. To further illustrate this, let me tell you a little about functions in programming. Functions are pieces of code that take in user data and then give output based on the instructions in the code. So the reason I am mentioning functions is because functions are endless and giving out user data is just a by-product, but we barely notice this as we are only ever interested in the output. On the same level, let's say your goal is to become physically fit, then the best way to approach this is to make exercise a part of your daily eternal life and the results will come by as a by-product, like an output of an endless function.

Atomic Habits is the one book I would recommend to a friend, family or any person above 10 years old who is passionate about something and is looking to start small and grow big. In a world where almost everything is a distraction, it is best to re-design it to your advantage where the habits that matter to you are the easiest to do and those that are not good for you are the hardest to achieve. This book makes perfect use of different ideas to help craft and cultivate good habits and break bad ones. I highlighted a tonne of interesting ideas from the book and I cannot exhaust them all here. To sum this up, we tend to imitate the behaviour of the close, the many and the powerful and there is always a never-ending pressure to comply with these three groups; nothing sustains motivation better than belonging to the group, so finding the best ‘group’ for your desired habits and identity is crucial for your development. All that said, if you are seeking to be a reader, this book would be a great start for you. (Hit me up for the E-book version for free).
